## The Routing Story: Why CN2 GIA, 9929, and CMIN2 Actually Matter

If you have ever run a traceroute from a Chinese ISP into a generic US VPS, you have probably seen paths that route through hop 12+ with 250ms+ latency and packet loss during peak hours. The reason is that the "default" international transit between China and the US goes through congested public peering points. The three premium routes ZgoCloud uses bypass that congestion by purchasing dedicated capacity:

**CN2 GIA (Global Internet Access)** is China Telecom's premium product, originally built for enterprise VPN and high-end commercial traffic. It uses fewer hops, dedicated bandwidth, and is the most expensive transit into China. ZgoCloud's LA AMD Optimised line uses CN2 GIA for inbound China Telecom traffic.

**AS9929 (China Unicom 169 Backbone Premium)** is Unicom's premium tier. The regular Unicom route (AS4837) is famously oversubscribed; AS9929 is the same network but on the premium backbone with priority scheduling. The LA AMD VPS, AMD Optimised, Intel Performance, and Ryzen9 lines all use 9929 for inbound Unicom traffic.

**CMIN2 (China Mobile International Network 2)** is the successor to CMIN1, with dramatically better peering and capacity. ZgoCloud uses CMIN2 for inbound China Mobile traffic on most LA plans.

The Osaka plans do not need these tricks — Japan is geographically close, and IIJ has excellent direct peering with all three Chinese carriers, which is why the Osaka EPYC and Ryzen9 lines are extremely popular with users who prioritize latency over raw bandwidth.

The Hong Kong and Tokyo plans use BGP with CN2 inbound, which gives clean China Telecom entry without the premium CMIN2/9929 paths — a more affordable middle ground.
